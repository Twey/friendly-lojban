III.I. Names

III.I.I. Lojbanisation

You may have noticed that the sounds given in the previous chapter do not cover every sound in English. In fact, given that Lojban aims to be culturally neutral, this would be extremely difficult: it would have to include every sound from every language available, or at least a large selection of the most popular ones! So, how do we represent foreign words in Lojban? Well, it turns out that no language has enough sounds to perfectly represent every sound in every other language. Lojban does what other languages do when importing a word from another language: it changes the word to fit the sounds available. For example, the Japanese word 東京 is commonly ‘Romanised’ as Tokyo. Likewise, the same word would be ‘Lojbanised’ as .tokios..

Lojban places a couple of additional constraints upon foreign words and names. Firstly, they must end with a consonant. This is to distinguish them from native words that they might otherwise resemble and make them immediately obvious to the reader or listener as non-native words that need not have meaning prescribed to them. There is an unspoken convention that the default consonant used for this purpose is ‘s’, but you may use whatever consonant you like; many people prefer an ‘r’ after ‘ah’ sounds as in cha (Lojbanised: .tcar.), and the most common Lojbanisation of Esperanto (a language) is .esperanton., since this is a valid declension of the word in Esperanto itself. Secondly, they must have pauses (written with a dot) on either side. This separates the word from the surrounding Lojban text, and prevents it from accidentally running together and being misinterpreted as part of one of the words to either side.

III.I.II. Verbatim Foreign Names

But what if you want to talk about a name literally, as it is pronounced and written in its native language, as when we discussed 東京 above? Well, to keep it separate from the rest of the text and indicate that it is a name, you must say “la'o gy. John .gy.” where John is your name. The ‘gy.’ used here can be any single Lojban word, so long as it does not appear within the name itself. Convention uses the name of the first letter of the language name to indicate the language in which the text is being conveyed; the Lojban word for English is glico. The Japanese name of Tokyo, for example, would conventionally be quoted as la'o py. 東京 .py., from the Lojban word for Japanese, which is ponjo. All this quoting is obviously quite clumsy, and there's no guarantee that other Lojbanists will know how to read or pronounce your name in your language, so most Lojbanists choose to Lojbanise their names or otherwise choose a Lojban name, for the convenience of everyone.

III.II. Greetings

So what can you do with these names? Well, as you'll see later, you can use them as part of an ordinary sentence. Here, though, we'll just concentrate on some basic phrases covered by a group of words collectively known as vocatives. The most useful of these is probably coi, which simply means hello.

Example III.1. The Use of coi

LojbanEnglish
coiHello.
coi .djon.Hello, John.

As you can see, coi can be used with the name of the person you are greeting, or on its own to address anybody listening, much as with English hello. All vocatives follow this pattern. Some other useful vocatives you'll probably encounter a lot are co'o ‘goodbye’, fi'i ‘welcome’ (as in “welcome to my home”, not as in “you're welcome” in response to “thank you”), ki'e ‘thank you’, and je'e ‘O.K./that's all right/you're welcome’.

Example III.2. Further Vocatives

LojbanEnglish
fi'iWelcome!
ki'e .djein.Thank you, Jane.
je'eNo problem.
co'o .djon.Goodbye, John.

To translate this chapter's title, we need only one more vocative, that you probably won't encounter very often, but which is very useful when meeting someone for the first time: mi'e, which basically means ‘I'm…’, introducing yourself.

Example III.3. A First Meeting

LojbanEnglish
coi .i mi'e .djon.Hi, I'm John.
coi .djon. .i mi'e .djein.Hello, John. I'm Jane.
